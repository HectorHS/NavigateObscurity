Last updated 02/05/2020.

Below you can see a snapshot of the current situation. Due to some data gathering lag, the data is about 1-2 days older than the last update date. The 4 main metrics are the confirmed coronavirus cases (Confirmed), deaths following a coronavirus infection (Deaths), complete recoveries following a coronavirus infection (Recovered), and currently infected people (Active), i.e. confirmed cases that have not been fatal, nor have they recovered yet. Apart from absolute numbers, I am also providing these metrics per 1 million people to get a better idea of how impacted a country is. 1000 deaths in a country with a 5m population means something very different to the same number of deaths in a country with a 500m population. Lastly, I am providing two rates, death rate: the ratio of cases that develop to fatalities, and recovery rate: the ratio of recovered patients to the total cases.

Note that these numbers give us a decent overview of the situation, but are still far from accurate. The number of confirmed cases depends heavily on the extend of testing each country deploys and that varies widely. In addition, there is mounting evidence that a large proportion of infected people never develop any symptoms (around 50% in Iceland, 50% in an Italian example, 30% at a Japanese case study, 18% in Princess Diamond), which means it’s less likely to even think to get tested. All this tells us that the actual cases are much more numerous than the confirmed cases (a study argues that about 86% of cases where undetected in pre lockdown Wuhan). The number of deaths, however, should be much more accurate.

The map above gives us a snapshot of the situation and a comparative view for the different countries, but does not help us understand how the epidemic is progressing. For that, we will need to track and visualise change. In the charts below you can see day by day change in two ways. On the top chart you can see the accumulating numbers for the different parameters. Visualised are the “active”, “recovered” and “death” cases, which when added up make up the "confirmed" cases. So, the whole visualised area represents the total confirmed cases. Since the numbers are accumulating, the total area will only go up, so the points of interest here are how steep the increase is, and when the “active” cases number starts flattening out and eventually reducing being replaced by “recovered” cases.

On the bottom chart, you can see the day by day (non accumulative) change on the same metrics. The new “confirmed” cases minus the new “recovered” cases and “death” cases gives us the new “active” cases. So, this chart can be read as a change of “active” cases per day. The first positive indication here is the flattening of new “confirmed” cases, and then eventually, having more “recoveries” than “confirmed” cases in a daily basis. You might see the background of the chart changing colours. This reflects a change in the government response regarding restrictions of movement. What each colour means is shown on the legend, and further information on each category can be found on the section below where a map is provided for the different government responses.

Hopefully it will all make more sense visually. To see what a hopeful visualisation looks like, just select China, or South Korea (Korea, South) from the dropdown. For a less hopeful visualisation have a look at USA.

Wherever you were, chances are that sometime during March or April you were told to stay home. Some governments merely outlined recommendations for the public, but most enforced rules to make citizens comply. Here you can see a rough visualization of government responses when it came to public movement restriction. For simplicity I am only using 4 broad categories of responses, but you can find more specific information by hovering over each country. The four categories range from no restrictions imposed (although recommendations are always made), to movement restrictions only in parts of the country, to restrictions for the whole country but only in particular times (curfew), to restrictions in the whole country all of the time (lockdown). Of course, many more government responses were put in place at the same time like banning gatherings, imposing social distancing rules, closing businesses etc, but the scope of this visualisation remains solely the movement restriction policies.
